# Strait of Hormuz Crisis Escalates as Trump Issues Ultimatum, Iran Rejects, Global Oil Markets Roil

> **Grounding Score: 100% — Verified**
> Desk: Politics | Published: Mon Apr 06 2026



---

US-Iran tensions reach boiling point in April 2026 after President Trump's ultimatum on the Strait of Hormuz, met with swift Iranian defiance, sending shockwaves through global oil markets and drawing international condemnation.

Washington, D.C. – Tensions in the Middle East reached a critical peak in early April 2026 as U.S. President Donald Trump delivered a stark ultimatum to Iran regarding the vital Strait of Hormuz, demanding its immediate reopening for international shipping or face severe consequences. Tehran, through its Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), swiftly rejected the demand, warning that the waterway would "never return to its former state, especially for the US and Israel," sending global oil prices soaring and sparking urgent calls for de-escalation from world leaders.

The crisis intensified following weeks of heightened military engagements in the region. President Trump's ultimatum, conveyed via a social media post on Truth Social, set a "Tuesday" deadline, threatening to strike Iranian power plants and bridges if the strait remained restricted. Iran's Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf condemned Trump's remarks as "reckless" and accused Washington of being driven by Israeli interests, stating such actions would drag the U.S. into "a living HELL." General Ali Abdollahi Aliabadi of Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters echoed the defiance, describing Trump's threats as "a helpless, nervous, unbalanced and stupid action" and warning that "the gates of hell will open for you."

The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow choke point between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman, is strategically critical, with approximately 20% to 25% of the world's liquefied natural gas (LNG) and 25% of seaborne oil trade passing through it annually. It serves as the primary export route for oil from major producers like Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, and Iran, making it a vital artery for the global economy. A sustained disruption could significantly impact global energy security, particularly for Asian economies such as China, India, and Japan, which are heavily reliant on these shipments. The current effective closure, ongoing since late February 2026 following joint U.S.-Israeli military strikes on Iran, has already been likened to the 1970s oil crisis.

The immediate impact on global oil markets has been severe. Brent crude prices surged, exceeding $100 per barrel in early March and reaching $126 per barrel at their peak, marking the fastest rise during any conflict in recent history. Regular gasoline prices in the United States have already increased by approximately 37% since the conflict began. Analysts from the International Energy Agency (IEA) have warned of worsening supply constraints in April as pre-war crude oil shipments are depleted, forecasting a significant spike in prices and potential physical shortages if the disruption is prolonged. Shipping firms have largely suspended operations in the area, with tanker traffic dropping sharply, further exacerbating concerns over global supply chains.

The international community has responded with widespread alarm, urging de-escalation. British Prime Minister Keir Starmer's office stated the UK is collaborating with allies to ensure the safety of commercial shipping and emphasized the importance of freedom of navigation. European Union leaders have condemned threats against civilian infrastructure, calling such actions "illegal." Meanwhile, diplomatic efforts continue in the background, with mediators having presented a 45-day ceasefire proposal to both the U.S. and Iran. While Iran's foreign ministry spokesperson confirmed receipt and indicated a formal response, they maintained that negotiations were "incompatible with ultimatums and threats to commit war crimes."

As the Tuesday deadline looms, the world watches with bated breath. The implications of a full military confrontation over the Strait of Hormuz are dire, threatening not only a catastrophic economic fallout but also a broader regional conflict. The ongoing crisis underscores the fragility of global energy markets and the persistent geopolitical tensions that define US-Iran relations, highlighting the urgent need for a diplomatic off-ramp to prevent further escalation.
