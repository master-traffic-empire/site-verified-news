# Trump Issues Ultimatum Amid Escalating Iran Conflict 2026

> **Grounding Score: 100% — Verified**
> Desk: Politics | Published: Tue Apr 07 2026

_President Trump threatens to bomb Iran power plants and bridges as 8 p.m. ET deadline looms for Strait of Hormuz reopening. Iran rejects ceasefire on Day 38._


---

**WASHINGTON D.C. –** With a critical deadline fast approaching, President Donald Trump has escalated his threats against Iran, vowing to bomb civilian power plants and bridges if Tehran does not reopen the Strait of Hormuz by 8 p.m. Eastern Time on Tuesday, April 7, 2026. The ultimatum comes on Day 38 of a protracted conflict, just hours after Iran formally rejected a proposed 45-day ceasefire, insisting on a permanent end to hostilities and guarantees against future attacks.

In a series of stark warnings issued over the weekend and into Tuesday, President Trump declared, "Tuesday will be Power Plant Day, and Bridge Day, all wrapped up in one, in Iran. There will be nothing like it!!!" He further asserted on Truth Social that "A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again," should Iran fail to meet his demands. These threats have drawn widespread international condemnation, with human rights organizations and United Nations Secretary-General António Guterres warning that targeting civilian infrastructure could constitute war crimes under international humanitarian law. President Trump, however, has dismissed these concerns, stating he is "not at all" worried about committing war crimes.

Iran's Foreign Ministry conveyed its rejection of the temporary ceasefire proposal through Pakistani mediators on Monday, April 6, emphasizing that Tehran seeks a definitive conclusion to the conflict and robust assurances against future aggression. Mojtaba Ferdousi Pour, head of the Iranian diplomatic mission in Cairo, explicitly told The Associated Press, "We won't merely accept a ceasefire. We only accept an end of the war with guarantees that we won't be attacked again." In a show of defiance against the U.S. threats, Iranian officials have reportedly urged young people to form human chains around power plants to protect them from potential strikes.

The current crisis stems from a broader conflict that erupted on February 28, 2026, following joint military strikes by the United States and Israel on Iran, which included the killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. In retaliation, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) issued warnings that effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz, a vital global chokepoint through which approximately 20% of the world's daily oil supply typically transits. The prolonged disruption of shipping traffic in the Strait has triggered severe supply shocks in the global oil market, with Brent Crude prices soaring above $110 a barrel on Tuesday.

Mediators from Pakistan, Egypt, and Turkey have been engaged in intense diplomatic efforts, "racing against time" to broker a compromise. However, the latest 45-day ceasefire framework, which sought an immediate cessation of hostilities and the reopening of the Strait, was met with Iran's firm rejection. Meanwhile, military actions continue to intensify, with both U.S. and Israeli forces conducting ongoing strikes against Iranian military targets and infrastructure. Israel's military, for instance, acknowledged on Tuesday striking eight bridges in Iran, claiming they were used for transporting military equipment. The U.S. has also hit Iran's key oil export terminal on Kharg Island.

As the deadline looms, the international community watches with bated breath, concerned about the potential for catastrophic escalation. President Trump indicated on Monday that he still believes Iran is negotiating "in good faith" and referred to Tehran's 10-point proposal as a "significant step," though "not good enough." However, his public declarations of imminent strikes paint a grim picture, raising fears of widespread devastation and further destabilization in an already volatile region. The implications of a full-scale assault on Iran's civilian infrastructure would be far-reaching, affecting global energy markets, humanitarian conditions, and the delicate balance of power in the Middle East.
